# 7 Reasons Your Solar Contract May Be Legally Cancellable (And What to Do About It)

*Short, shareable guide — ideal for Substack, Facebook Groups, and community forums*

---

Hundreds of thousands of American homeowners are currently trapped in solar contracts they regret. If you are one of them, here is something the solar company does not want you to know: **you may have the legal right to cancel your contract entirely.**

Here are the seven most common grounds that consumer protection attorneys use to successfully cancel solar contracts:

---

### 1. Your Savings Were Misrepresented

If the salesperson showed you a projection saying you would save $150, $200, or $300 per month — and those savings never happened — that may constitute fraudulent misrepresentation under your state's consumer protection law.

**What to do:** Pull your utility bills from before and after installation. If your actual savings are significantly below what was projected in writing, document the gap. This is often the strongest evidence in a cancellation case.

---

### 2. Your Financing Documents Contain TILA Violations

The Truth in Lending Act (TILA) requires specific disclosures in consumer credit agreements. Solar loans — through companies like GoodLeap, Mosaic, Loanpal, and Sunnova — are frequently originated with incomplete or incorrect disclosures. A TILA violation can give you the right to rescind the entire transaction.

**What to do:** Have a consumer protection attorney review your loan documents. TILA violations are often technical and not obvious to non-attorneys, but they are common in solar financing.

---

### 3. You Were Never Given Your FTC Right to Cancel

The FTC's Cooling-Off Rule gives you three business days to cancel any contract signed at your home. The sales representative is legally required to give you written notice of this right at the time of signing. If they did not, the rescission window may never have legally started — meaning it may still be open today.

**What to do:** Look through your contract documents for a "Notice of Right to Cancel" form. If you do not have one, mention this to an attorney immediately.

---

### 4. Your Contract Has Hidden Escalator Clauses

Many solar contracts contain provisions that increase your monthly payments by 2–3% per year for the life of the contract. If these escalators were not clearly explained to you during the sales process, they may constitute a material omission under consumer protection law.

**What to do:** Read your contract carefully and look for language about "annual payment increases," "escalation rates," or "rate adjustments." If you find them and were not told about them, document this.

---

### 5. You Were Told You Would Get a Tax Credit You Did Not Qualify For

The federal solar tax credit (ITC) is worth 30% of the system cost — but it only applies if you have sufficient federal tax liability. Many homeowners were told they would receive this credit when they were not actually eligible. If this representation was a factor in your decision to sign, you may have a claim for fraudulent inducement.

**What to do:** Review your tax returns from the year of installation. If you did not receive the credit and were told you would, document the original representation.

---

### 6. Your System Consistently Underperforms

Solar contracts typically include a production guarantee — a minimum amount of electricity the system is contractually obligated to produce. If your system has consistently produced less than the guaranteed amount, you have grounds for breach of contract.

**What to do:** Pull your system's production reports (available through the monitoring app or portal) and compare them to the production guarantee in your contract. Any consistent shortfall is documented evidence of breach.

---

### 7. Your Solar Company Went Bankrupt or Sold Your Contract

If your original solar company has gone bankrupt (Pink Energy, Sungevity, and others have) or sold your contract to a third party, you may have grounds to challenge the enforceability of the agreement. Bankruptcy proceedings sometimes create opportunities to exit contracts that would otherwise be binding.

**What to do:** Research the current status of your solar company. If it has filed for bankruptcy or been acquired, contact a consumer protection attorney immediately.
