## Q&A 1

**Question:** Can I cancel a solar lease if the company lied about my savings?

**Answer:**
Yes — potentially. Misrepresentation of savings is one of the most common grounds for solar contract cancellation, and it is specifically addressed by the FTC and most state consumer protection laws.

Here is the legal framework:

**Federal level:** The FTC Act prohibits unfair or deceptive acts in commerce. Promising savings that the system cannot deliver is a deceptive act.

**State level:** Every state has some version of a Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA). In Texas, for example, the DTPA allows consumers to sue for 3x damages plus attorney fees when a seller makes false representations.

**Contract law:** If you were induced to sign based on false representations, the doctrine of fraudulent inducement may allow you to void the contract entirely.

The practical path: get a free legal review of your specific contract and situation. A consumer protection attorney can identify which specific violations apply to your case. breakyoursolarcontract.com offers this at no cost.

## Q&A 2

**Question:** What happens to a solar lease when you sell your house?

**Answer:**
This is one of the most important questions solar homeowners don't ask until it's too late.

When you sell a home with a solar lease or PPA, you have three options:

1. **Transfer the lease to the buyer** — The buyer must qualify with the solar company and agree to assume the remaining term. Many buyers refuse, especially FHA/VA buyers who often cannot assume solar leases under their loan guidelines.

2. **Buy out the lease** — Pay the net present value of remaining payments. This typically ranges from $15,000 to $45,000 depending on your contract and how many years remain.

3. **Cancel the lease through legal grounds** — If the solar company violated consumer protection laws or made deceptive representations, you may be able to cancel without paying the buyout. This is the best option when available.

The critical thing to know: a solar lease is recorded as a lien on your property title. It shows up in every title search and must be resolved before closing. Many real estate transactions have fallen apart because of solar lease complications.

If you are planning to sell and have a solar lease, I'd strongly recommend getting a free legal review at breakyoursolarcontract.com before you list. Knowing your options before you accept an offer gives you much more leverage.
