## The Scale of This Problem Is Enormous

What happened to me is not unusual. According to consumer protection attorneys who specialize in this area, solar contract disputes have become one of the fastest-growing categories of consumer legal complaints in the United States.

The Federal Trade Commission has received tens of thousands of complaints about solar companies. State attorneys general in California, Florida, Texas, Arizona, and New Jersey have launched investigations into deceptive solar sales practices. Companies like Sunrun, SunPower, Vivint Solar, ADT Solar, and Freedom Forever have all faced class action lawsuits alleging systematic misrepresentation.

The complaints are remarkably consistent across the country:

- Savings projections that were wildly optimistic and never materialized
- Escalating monthly payments that were buried in the fine print
- Federal tax credit representations made to homeowners who were not actually eligible
- Systems that consistently underperformed their contractual production guarantees
- Liens placed on homes that complicated refinancing and sales
- Companies that went bankrupt, leaving homeowners with orphaned systems and no warranty support

## What I Wish I Had Known Before I Signed

**Solar leases and PPAs are not the same as owning solar.** When you sign a Power Purchase Agreement or a solar lease, you do not own the system on your roof. You are renting it — for 20 to 25 years. The solar company owns the system, claims the tax credits, and you pay them for the electricity it generates. If the system underperforms, you may still owe the full contracted amount.

**Solar loans are different — but come with their own problems.** If you financed your solar system through a loan (from companies like GoodLeap, Mosaic, Loanpal, or Sunnova), you own the system but are responsible for the debt. These loans are subject to the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), and many were originated with incomplete or incorrect disclosures — which can give you legal grounds to rescind.

**The FTC gives you three days to cancel any contract signed at your home.** This is called the Cooling-Off Rule. The sales representative is legally required to give you written notice of this right at the time of signing. If they did not — and many did not — the rescission window may still be legally open, even years later.

**Your state has consumer protection laws that apply to solar sales.** Every state has statutes that prohibit deceptive trade practices. In many states — California, Texas, Florida, New Jersey, Massachusetts, North Carolina — these laws allow homeowners to recover actual damages, attorney fees, and in some cases, multiple times their actual damages.

## The Legal Grounds That Actually Work

After spending months researching this and eventually working with a consumer protection attorney, I learned that the most successful grounds for solar contract cancellation are:

**Misrepresentation of savings.** If you were shown specific savings projections during the sales process — "you'll save $200 a month" — and those projections turned out to be significantly overstated, that may constitute fraudulent misrepresentation or a violation of your state's consumer protection law.

**TILA violations.** The Truth in Lending Act requires specific disclosures in consumer credit agreements. Solar loan documents frequently contain errors or omissions that constitute TILA violations — and a TILA violation can give you the right to rescind the entire transaction.

**Failure to disclose the right of rescission.** If the sales representative did not provide you with written notice of your FTC three-day right to cancel, the rescission period may never have legally started — meaning it may still be open.

**Undisclosed escalator clauses.** Many solar contracts contain provisions that increase your payments by 2–3% per year. If these were not clearly disclosed and explained during the sales process, they may constitute a material omission under consumer protection law.

**Misrepresentation of the tax credit.** If you were told you would receive a 30% federal tax credit and you were not actually eligible for it (because you do not have sufficient federal tax liability), and this representation influenced your decision to sign, you may have a claim for fraudulent inducement.

## How the Cancellation Process Actually Works

Here is what a typical solar contract cancellation case looks like:

The process begins with a free consultation with a consumer protection attorney who specializes in solar contracts. During this consultation, the attorney reviews your contract, financing documents, and any written communications from the sales representative. This typically takes about an hour and costs nothing.

If the attorney identifies viable grounds for cancellation, they send a formal demand letter to the solar company. This letter asserts the legal grounds for cancellation, demands release of the contract, and demands removal of any lien on your property.

Most cases — the majority, in fact — resolve through negotiation rather than litigation. Solar companies have legal departments that regularly settle these disputes. They would rather release a contract than face a lawsuit that could expose them to treble damages and attorney fees under state consumer protection laws.

The typical timeline from initial consultation to resolution is 30 to 90 days. In my case, it took 67 days from the first call to the attorney to receiving a signed cancellation agreement from the solar company.

## What You Should Do Today

If you are currently in a solar contract and believe you were misled, here is your action plan:

**First, gather your documents.** You need your original solar contract, your financing agreement (if applicable), any written proposals or savings estimates you received during the sales process, and your utility bills from the 12 months before and after installation. The comparison between projected and actual savings is often the most powerful evidence.

**Second, do not sign anything the solar company sends you.** Companies sometimes send modification agreements or "goodwill" offers that contain language waiving your right to pursue cancellation. Do not sign anything without having an attorney review it first.

**Third, get a free legal review.** Consumer protection attorneys who specialize in solar contracts typically offer free initial consultations. There is no cost to find out whether you have a case. If you do, many of these attorneys work on a contingency basis — meaning they only get paid if you win.
